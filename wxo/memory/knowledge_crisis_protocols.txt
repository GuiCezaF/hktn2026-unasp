# Crisis Response Protocols: Landslide Events

## Overview
This document outlines the standard operating procedures, severity classification criteria, and volunteer skill requirements for coordinating responses to landslide incidents. It is intended to serve as the primary knowledge base for the CrisisCoordinator agent in the watsonx Orchestrate environment.

## 0. Mensagens urgentes e relatos humanos (prioridade máxima)

Quando o texto do usuário indicar **risco imediato à vida** — por exemplo: deslizamento, terra caindo, prédio ou casa **rangendo**, rachaduras que pioram, **desabamento** iminente, **“tem gente dentro”**, pessoas presas, feridos, gritos de socorro, mensagens curtas com erros de digitação ou sem pontuação:

1. **Não bloqueie o fluxo com checklist** (número exato da casa, CEP, quantidade precisa de pessoas, fotos ou links). Quem relata pode estar em pânico, com pouca bateria ou sinal instável.
2. **Infira** local a partir de **qualquer fragmento** útil: rua, bairro, cidade, ponto de referência (“perto da padaria”, “bloco verde”). Se só houver texto solto, use-o como `location` no registro do incidente; refine depois se novas mensagens chegarem.
3. **Na mesma conversa**, sem esperar “tudo preenchido”: classifique a severidade, **registre o incidente** com os dados inferidos e **busque voluntários** compatíveis. Ações de backoffice vêm antes de pedir mais detalhes.
4. **Pergunta única e mínima** apenas quando **não existir nenhuma pista geográfica** (nem bairro, nem cidade, nem nome de lugar). Uma pergunta curta; nunca uma lista de três ou mais itens obrigatórios antes de agir.
5. **Tom da resposta:** uma frase de acolhimento; em seguida confirme **em linguagem natural** o que foi entendido (local aproximado, tipo de risco); declare que **o registro e a mobilização já foram iniciados** ou estão em curso.

Palavras soltas (“ajuda”, “por favor”, “tá caindo”) somadas a um bairro ou rua já são informação suficiente para o primeiro ciclo de ferramentas.

## 1. Severity Classification Criteria

Incidents should be classified into one of three severity levels based on the reported conditions:

### Critical (Crítico)
* **Definition:** Immediate threat to life, active structural collapse, or people trapped/missing.
* **Triggers:**
  * Reports of injuries or missing persons.
  * Active landslides currently moving.
  * Collapsed homes or infrastructure.
  * Blocked primary evacuation routes.
* **Expected Action:** Immediate dispatch of Search & Rescue and Medical (First Aid) volunteers. High-priority notification.

### Moderate (Moderado)
* **Definition:** High risk of escalation, imminent threat to structures, but no immediate reports of trapped individuals.
* **Triggers:**
  * Cracks appearing in walls, roads, or terrain.
  * Slanted trees or utility poles.
  * Evacuation of homes in progress or required.
  * Heavy continuous rainfall in known risk areas.
* **Expected Action:** Dispatch Structural Engineering, Drone Operators, and Logistics volunteers to assess the area and support evacuation.

### Low (Baixo)
* **Definition:** Preventative situations, minor landslides with no immediate threat to life or primary structures.
* **Triggers:**
  * Minor mud flow blocking secondary roads.
  * Fallen debris that does not affect housing.
  * Requests for post-incident clean-up or monitoring.
* **Expected Action:** Dispatch Logistics, Communication, and GIS Mapping volunteers for assessment and clean-up coordination.

## 2. Volunteer Skill Categories

The system uses specific skill identifiers to match volunteers to incidents. Below is the official list of recognized skills:

* **primeiros socorros:** Medical assistance, triage, CPR, and basic life support for injured individuals.
* **logística:** Coordination of supplies (food, water, blankets), transport organization, and managing distribution centers.
* **operador de drone:** Aerial reconnaissance, assessing terrain stability, and locating blocked routes or missing persons from the air.
* **mapeamento gis:** Geographic Information Systems mapping, analyzing topography, and updating risk area maps based on real-time data.
* **apoio psicológico:** Crisis counseling, comforting victims, and providing mental health first aid to affected families.
* **engenharia estrutural:** Assessing the stability of buildings, retaining walls, and infrastructure to determine if they are safe to enter or need immediate shoring.
* **busca e resgate:** Specialized physical rescue operations, locating and extracting trapped individuals safely.
* **comunicação:** Disseminating accurate information, coordinating with local media, and operating radio/communication equipment in areas with poor cellular signal.

## 3. Standard Operating Procedures (SOP)

When a new incident is reported, the following steps must be taken:

1. **Information gathering (infer-first):** Extract whatever location and risk signals exist, including partial or informal phrasing. For **critical** signals (Section 0), treat missing numbers or media as normal; do not delay tools waiting for “complete” data.
2. **Severity Assessment:** Apply the Severity Classification Criteria (Critical, Moderate, Low) based on the gathered or inferred information.
3. **Skill Identification:** Determine the top 2-3 most critical volunteer skills required for the initial response based on the severity and specific details of the incident.
4. **Volunteer Matching:** Use the matching system to find available volunteers who possess the required skills and are geographically close to the incident.
5. **Notification:** Alert the selected volunteers with a concise summary of the incident, required actions, and safety protocols.

## 4. Safety Protocols for Volunteers

* **Do not enter unstable structures:** Only certified structural engineers or search & rescue teams should enter potentially compromised buildings.
* **Maintain communication:** All deployed volunteers must check in every hour.
* **Evacuate if conditions worsen:** If rainfall intensifies or earth movement is detected, evacuate the risk area immediately.
